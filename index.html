Section 01

Short Bio

Alla Vikhoreva is a figurative painter working in oil, charcoal, pastel, and drawing, based in Olympia, Washington, where she has lived for ten years. Born in Russia, she was introduced to oil painting as a young child by her grandfather, a painter who lived at the center of a creative world of artists and musicians in Krasnoyarsk, Siberia. Her current practice is guided by ongoing mentorship with Zhenya Gershman, a painter and art historian who has taught at the J. Paul Getty Museum and created commissioned portraits for the GRAMMY Foundation. Gershman's approach — rooted in the old masters while developing each artist's individual vision — has been formative for Vikhoreva's work.

Longer Bio

Working across oil, charcoal, pastel, and drawing, Vikhoreva moves between portraiture, figure studies, landscapes, and intimate observational works, guided by an interest in atmosphere, mystery, and emotional presence. Rather than directing the viewer toward a fixed interpretation, she approaches painting as an open encounter — one that invites stillness, questioning, and personal recognition.

Born in Russia, Vikhoreva grew up immersed in classical art and music — experiences she regards not as privilege but as a birthright that should be available to everyone. Her formal education and early professional life led her into management, economics, and accounting — a path that brought her to the United States on her own. Art remained a constant undercurrent and became central again after she settled in Washington State. Her current practice is guided by ongoing mentorship with Zhenya Gershman, a painter and art historian who has taught at the J. Paul Getty Museum and created commissioned portraits for the GRAMMY Foundation. Gershman's approach — rooted in the old masters while developing each artist's individual vision — has been formative for Vikhoreva's work.

Section 02

Artist Statement

My grandfather was a painter. He was also a man who lived at the center of a creative world — artists, musicians, the whole culture of making and sharing beauty was the air in his home. When I was three years old, he started taking me on plein airs during the summer. He was the first person to put oil paint in my hands. He died when I was five, but the door he opened never closed.

What he gave me, without ever explaining it, was the experience of being nourished by art. Not taught, not tested — nourished. That experience became my compass. It told me something I still believe: art, at its best, is not something you need to understand. It is something that makes you more human.

I grew up where classical art and music were woven into the rhythm of ordinary life. Paintings, concerts, museums — these were my childhood, available and natural. That exposure shaped how I learned to see. It broadened my sense of what a human being can be — not a citizen of one country, but a person of the whole earth, capable of seeing and hearing even when the language is unknown.

When I moved to the United States on my own and settled in Olympia, Washington — where I have now lived for ten years — I found a region overflowing with creativity. Artists, studios, galleries, contemporary work in every town. The Pacific Northwest is a remarkable place for making and sharing art. What I noticed, though, was that the emphasis lives strongly in the contemporary — and the classical traditions that shaped me, the old masters, the centuries of figurative painting, the history that gives roots to everything we create today, were harder to encounter in an approachable way. I would love to bring these two worlds closer together. Not to replace what is here, but to deepen it — and to be part of that connection.

I paint because I believe a painting is a conversation that needs no common language. When I stand before a Titian, I feel something he may have intended — and that is the miracle. Five hundred years dissolve. I am in dialogue with an artist I will never meet, and what passes between us may be exactly what was meant to pass. That exchange — across centuries, across cultures, without a single word — is proof that art unites us in ways nothing else can. It connects nations. It teaches us to observe, to listen, to feel across every boundary we have built. It is the most honest language I know.

My work is shaped with oil, charcoal, and pastel. I paint and draw figures, portraits, hands, landscapes — whatever carries the weight of the inner world into visible form. Technically, I am drawn to atmosphere: forms that emerge from shadow, restrained palettes, surfaces that ask the viewer to slow down and stay. But technique is the vessel, not the purpose. The purpose is what the painting can offer the person who encounters it.

I believe every viewer sees only what they need to see in that moment. You can return to the same painting over the years, and each time it gives you something different — because you yourself have changed. This is the deepest generosity of visual art. Unlike words, which can build walls between languages and cultures, a painting binds us. It has no age, no nationality, no single meaning. It meets each viewer exactly where they are, and it offers without demanding.

The question I carry into the studio every day is simple: How can I be honest with the world, so that others can feel? If I am honest enough — with the light, the color, the subject, the emotion — the painting will do the rest. It will find the person who needs it. It will offer them a moment of stillness, or recognition, or beauty they did not expect. In a world of increasing isolation and division, I believe painting and drawing rooted in the figurative tradition, made with genuine care, have become urgent. Not as decoration, not as intellectual exercise, but as a gift — offered freely, the way my grandfather offered me those summer plein airs. Without explanation, without condition, trusting that the experience itself would do the work.

This is why I paint: not because of what it says about me, but because of what it can give to someone else.

Section 03

Why Seattle / Why Now

This fellowship feels meaningful to me because it brings together several things I care about deeply: sustained time for painting, serious commitment to technical growth, and a philosophy that sees art not as a closed professional language but as something that can genuinely reach people and help them feel more connected to their own humanity.

I am at a moment in my life when painting is no longer peripheral to who I am — it is the work I want to deepen with full seriousness. The Seattle Prize's commitment to Beauty, Truth, and Love — to art created as a gift rather than a product — speaks directly to why I paint. I have always believed that the question is not "Is this art?" but "Does this help someone feel something real?"

Seattle also makes sense for me practically and artistically. I am already based in Washington State, and the possibility of developing my work within a focused painter's community while remaining connected to the region feels especially timely. What draws me most to the Seattle Prize is its vision of craftsmanship in service of human connection — and its conviction that great art should be accessible to everyone, not reserved for those who already know how to look.

Section 04

Project Proposals

Vessels — The Human Body as Sacred Ground

A series of oil paintings exploring the human body not as anatomy, but as landscape. Hands, shoulders, the bend of a spine, the curve of a neck — isolated from full figures, treated as vessels: deeply spiritual yet entirely physical, grounded in flesh and bone while reaching toward something we cannot name.

I would work in the tradition of Carrière's atmospheric softness — forms emerging from and dissolving into shadow — combined with tactile, grounded rendering of skin and surface. Restrained palette: earth tones, warm grays, flashes of light where the body catches it. Each painting would feel both ancient and immediate.

The body is the most universal thing we share, yet we rarely stop to regard it with reverence. This series invites viewers into a quiet confrontation with their own physicality — a reminder of the weight and wonder of being inside a body. In a culture that alternately commodifies and ignores the body, these paintings ask: When did you last look at your own hands with wonder?

Where the Veil Is Thin — Portraits and Landscapes from the Roof of the World

In 2025, I traveled to remote mountain villages in the Himalayas. The stillness, the altitude, the faces of people living at the edge of the sky — none of it has left me.

There is a reason so many spiritual traditions place their monasteries and temples at altitude. The higher you climb, the thinner the air becomes — and with it, the veil between the physical world and something larger seems to thin as well. Colors sharpen. Silence deepens. The boundary between what you can see and what you can only feel grows transparent. I experienced this firsthand, and I believe paint is the right medium to carry that feeling to others.

This series would combine landscape and portraiture: vast mountain panoramas rendered in oil, paired with intimate portraits of the villagers who live among them — their weathered hands, their steady gazes, the quiet endurance written in their faces. I have extensive 35mm film photographs from my travels as foundation, but the paintings would go where photographs cannot — into color, mood, and the emotional truth of standing at the boundary between worlds.

Not everyone can climb to a monastery at 15,000 feet. But everyone can stand before a painting and feel the altitude in their chest — that thinning of the veil, that sense of being closer to something vast and quiet. I want to bring the silence and dignity of these remote lives into a gallery where someone walking in off the street can encounter an entirely different way of being alive.

The Open Room — Art History as Living Practice

Alongside my studio work, I would develop a free monthly public program at the Occidental Fine Art Center.

Each gathering would be a single, continuous experience. Using high-resolution projections of masterworks — many now freely available through museum open-access programs like the Getty and the Met — I would guide attendees through a painting, an artist, or a movement. Not as academic instruction, but as shared encounter: What do you notice? What do you feel? Why do you think the artist made this choice?

Then, while the work is still fresh in the room, participants would be invited into a drawing exercise rooted in the technique they just studied — not to produce a finished piece, but to understand the masterwork through their own hand. What happens when you try what Carrière did with shadow? What do you notice about Toulouse-Lautrec's line once you've attempted it yourself? And then — the most important moment — they look at the original again, and see it completely differently. Because now their body knows something their eyes alone could not teach them.

This program would be a gathering point — a place where people who share a curiosity about art can meet each other, discover the classical tradition in an approachable way, and build connections through a shared experience of beauty. The Pacific Northwest is full of artists, makers, and curious people. What I want to offer is a space where the traditions I grew up in and the creative community already here can meet and deepen each other.

This community program would complement, not replace, my full-time studio practice.

Section 06

Positioning Notes

This packet presents a coherent figurative practice without forcing the work into an artificial series. The throughline is not sameness of subject, but seriousness of sensibility: atmosphere, emotional presence, and painting as an act of human connection and service.

For portfolio sequencing, the strongest order is: presence, skill, sensitivity, range, coherence. Lead with the most psychologically charged figure or portrait, then build through hands, portraiture, classical studies, landscape, and figure drawing.

The three project proposals are designed to work as a unified vision: two ambitious painting series (Vessels and Where the Veil Is Thin) demonstrating the scope of studio work, plus a community program (The Open Room) that directly aligns with the Conru Foundation's stated mission of providing free classical art engagement to the broader public.
