Section 01

Short Bio

Alla Vikhoreva is a self-taught figurative painter based in Washington State. Working across oil, charcoal, pastel, and drawing, she creates portraits, figure studies, landscapes, and intimate observational works united by an interest in atmosphere, mystery, and emotional presence.

Born in Russia and introduced to oil painting in early childhood by her grandfather, a painter, Vikhoreva’s practice is shaped by classical cultural memory, travel, spiritual inquiry, and ongoing mentorship with painter Zhanya Gershman.

Longer Bio

Working across oil, charcoal, pastel, and drawing, she moves between portraiture, figure studies, landscapes, and intimate object-based works, guided by an interest in atmosphere, mystery, and emotional truth. Rather than directing the viewer toward a fixed interpretation, she approaches painting as an open encounter—one that invites stillness, questioning, and personal recognition.

Born in Russia, Vikhoreva was introduced to oil painting in early childhood by her grandfather, a painter, and grew up with a strong awareness of classical art and music. Although her formal education and early professional life led her into management, economics, accounting, and audit, art remained a constant undercurrent and became central again after she moved to the United States on her own. Her current practice is shaped by close observation, cultural memory, extensive travel, spiritual study, and ongoing mentorship with painter Zhanya Gershman.

Section 02

Practice Statement

I think of painting as a form of conversation: between my inner world and the inner worlds of others, between the present and the past, and between the viewer and artists who lived centuries before us. What draws me to painting is its ability to create contact without requiring explanation. A person can stand before an image and feel recognized, moved, or changed by it, even across distance, culture, or time. That possibility feels especially important now, when so much of life is fast, mediated, and increasingly disconnected.

I work across portraiture, figure, landscape, and drawing, but what unites these subjects is not category. It is my search for atmosphere, emotional presence, and mystery. I am interested in works that slow a viewer down and invite a more inward kind of seeing. Whether I am painting a face, a hand, a body, a landscape, or a classical study, I want the work to remain open enough for a viewer to enter it personally and return to it differently over time.

My path has been nontraditional. I was born in Russia and introduced to oil painting in early childhood by my grandfather, a painter. Although my formal education and professional life initially took me into management, economics, and accounting, art remained a constant thread and became central again after I moved to the United States on my own. That path has made me especially aware of how essential art is to human life: not as decoration or status, but as a way of developing attention, sensitivity, memory, and a wider sense of what it means to be human.

I care deeply about technical growth because I believe skill gives an artist a greater capacity to communicate something real. I am committed to building that capacity through painting and drawing while making work that stays honest, emotionally resonant, and accessible to others.

Section 03

Why Seattle / Why Now

This fellowship feels meaningful to me because it brings together several things I care about deeply: sustained time for painting, serious commitment to technical growth, and a philosophy that sees art not as a closed professional language but as something that can genuinely reach people. I am at a moment in my life when painting is no longer peripheral to who I am—it is the work I want to deepen with full seriousness.

Seattle also makes sense for me practically and artistically. I am already based in Washington State, and the possibility of developing my work within a focused painter’s community while remaining connected to the region feels especially timely. What draws me most to Seattle Art Prize is its commitment to craftsmanship, human dignity, and work that can nourish rather than alienate.

Section 04

Project Ideas

Vessel

This project would explore the human body in fragments, treating individual parts not simply as anatomy but as vessels of memory, labor, tenderness, devotion, and interior life. I am interested in the tension between the body’s material groundedness and its spiritual charge—how a hand, a shoulder, a back, a neck, or a posture can hold emotional and symbolic meaning far beyond description.

Formally, I want to build this series through sustained work in oil and drawing, using close observation while allowing color, texture, and atmosphere to carry psychological weight. Some works may remain intimate in scale, while others may expand into larger compositions in which bodily fragments become almost landscape-like in their presence.

High Places

This project would grow from my experiences in Tibet and Nepal, where landscape, daily life, and spiritual atmosphere cannot be easily separated. I would like to develop a body of work that moves between mountain views, village life, portraits of local people, and scenes of ordinary presence within extraordinary terrain—not as documentary record, but as an attempt to translate the feeling of those places for viewers who may never encounter them directly.

I am interested in the relationship between scale and intimacy: the vastness of mountains alongside the small, human gestures that make life within them visible.

Resonance

This project would investigate how color itself can carry emotional and psychological states, moving between figuration and abstraction. I am interested in moments when form begins to loosen and color becomes the primary vehicle for atmosphere, vibration, and feeling.

Section 06

Positioning Notes

This packet is designed to present a coherent figurative practice without forcing the work into an artificial series. The throughline is not sameness of subject, but seriousness of sensibility: atmosphere, mystery, emotional presence, and painting as an act of human connection.

For portfolio sequencing, the strongest order is: presence, skill, sensitivity, range, coherence. Lead with the most psychologically charged figure or self-portrait, then build through hands, portraiture, classical studies, landscape, and figure drawing.
